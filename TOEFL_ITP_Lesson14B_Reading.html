Текст 1 — Oceanography
~260 слов · вопросы 1–10
📄 Прочитай перед ответами

The deep ocean, generally defined as water below 200 meters, remains one of the least explored environments on Earth. Despite covering more than sixty percent of the planet's surface, the deep sea was long considered inhospitable to life due to its extreme cold, crushing pressure, and complete absence of sunlight. The discovery of hydrothermal vents in 1977 fundamentally changed scientists' understanding of life's potential in extreme environments.

Hydrothermal vents are cracks in the seafloor through which superheated, mineral-rich water is expelled. Unlike surface ecosystems that depend on sunlight for energy, the ecosystems around hydrothermal vents are powered by a process called chemosynthesis. Bacteria at the base of the food chain convert chemicals from the vent water — particularly hydrogen sulfide — into organic matter. These bacteria support a surprisingly diverse community of organisms, including giant tube worms, clams, shrimp, and crabs.

The discovery of hydrothermal vent communities had profound implications for astrobiology — the study of the potential for life elsewhere in the universe. If life can thrive without sunlight in the extreme conditions of the deep sea, scientists reasoned, similar ecosystems might exist on other planetary bodies. Europa, one of Jupiter's moons, is considered a particularly promising candidate because it possesses a vast subsurface ocean kept liquid by tidal heating from Jupiter's gravity.

Research into deep-sea ecosystems remains challenging due to the technological requirements of deep-ocean exploration. However, advances in remotely operated vehicles and pressure-resistant equipment have progressively expanded our knowledge of this hidden world.

Текст 2 — History
~270 слов · вопросы 11–20
📄 Прочитай перед ответами

The Renaissance, which flourished in Europe between the fourteenth and seventeenth centuries, represented a profound cultural and intellectual transformation. The term itself, meaning "rebirth" in French, refers to the renewed interest in the art, philosophy, and literature of ancient Greece and Rome. This classical revival, combined with new humanist ideas placing human beings at the center of inquiry, produced some of the most celebrated works in Western civilization.

The Renaissance originated in the prosperous city-states of northern Italy, particularly Florence. Wealthy merchant families, most notably the Medici, became prominent patrons of the arts, sponsoring artists, architects, and scholars. This patronage system enabled talented individuals such as Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo to devote themselves entirely to creative and intellectual pursuits, producing works that continue to define the era.

A crucial development that accelerated the spread of Renaissance ideas was the invention of the printing press by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440. Before this invention, books were copied by hand and available only to the wealthy and educated elite. The printing press made texts far more accessible and affordable, enabling ideas to spread rapidly across Europe. Within decades, thousands of books were in circulation, dramatically expanding literacy and facilitating the exchange of knowledge.

The Renaissance also had a significant impact on scientific thinking. Scholars began to challenge the authority of ancient texts and the medieval Church, emphasizing direct observation and experimentation. This spirit of inquiry laid the groundwork for the Scientific Revolution of the seventeenth century, which would transform humanity's understanding of the natural world.

Текст 3 — Psychology
~265 слов · вопросы 21–30
📄 Прочитай перед ответами

The study of human motivation has been a central concern of psychology since the field's earliest days. One of the most influential frameworks for understanding motivation is Abraham Maslow's hierarchy of needs, first proposed in 1943. Maslow argued that human needs can be arranged in a hierarchical pyramid, with the most fundamental physiological needs at the base and more complex psychological needs at higher levels.

At the foundation of Maslow's pyramid are physiological needs — the basic requirements for survival, including food, water, sleep, and shelter. Above these are safety needs, encompassing physical security, employment, and financial stability. The third level consists of social needs, reflecting humans' innate desire for belonging, friendship, and intimate relationships. Esteem needs — the desire for achievement, recognition, and self-respect — occupy the fourth level. At the apex of the pyramid sits self-actualization, which Maslow described as the desire to realize one's full potential.

Maslow proposed that individuals generally pursue higher-level needs only after lower-level needs have been adequately satisfied. However, subsequent research has challenged this strict sequential interpretation. Studies have shown that people frequently pursue multiple needs simultaneously and that the prioritization of needs varies considerably across different cultures.

Despite these criticisms, Maslow's framework has proven remarkably durable and has been widely applied in fields ranging from education and management to healthcare. Its enduring influence lies partly in its intuitive appeal — the idea that human motivation is fundamentally layered and progressive resonates with many people's lived experiences.

Текст 4 — Environmental Science
~260 слов · вопросы 31–40
📄 Прочитай перед ответами

Soil degradation has emerged as one of the most pressing but underappreciated environmental challenges of the twenty-first century. Healthy soil is essential not only for food production but also for regulating water cycles, storing carbon, and supporting biodiversity. Yet human activities — including intensive agriculture, deforestation, and urban expansion — have significantly diminished soil quality across vast areas of the globe.

Intensive agricultural practices are among the primary drivers of soil degradation. The repeated use of heavy machinery compacts the soil, reducing its ability to absorb water and support plant roots. Excessive use of synthetic fertilizers and pesticides depletes natural soil microorganisms that are critical for nutrient cycling. Monoculture farming — the cultivation of a single crop over large areas — further reduces biodiversity and makes soils more vulnerable to erosion.

The consequences of soil degradation are severe and wide-ranging. Degraded soils produce lower crop yields, contributing to food insecurity in already vulnerable regions. The loss of topsoil through erosion can be nearly irreversible on human timescales, as the formation of just one centimeter of topsoil requires hundreds to thousands of years. Furthermore, degraded soils release stored carbon into the atmosphere, exacerbating climate change.

Addressing soil degradation requires a combination of policy changes and shifts in agricultural practice. Techniques such as crop rotation, reduced tillage, and the use of organic fertilizers have been shown to restore and maintain soil health. Many experts argue that protecting soil should be accorded the same level of priority as protecting forests and oceans in global environmental policy.

Текст 5 — Technology & Society
~255 слов · вопросы 41–50
📄 Прочитай перед ответами

Artificial intelligence (AI) is transforming industries and societies at a pace that many experts describe as unprecedented. Unlike earlier waves of automation, which primarily displaced routine physical labor, AI systems are increasingly capable of performing complex cognitive tasks — from diagnosing medical conditions and translating languages to generating creative content and making financial decisions.

The economic implications of AI are deeply contested. Optimists argue that AI will create new industries and jobs, just as previous technological revolutions did. They point to historical precedents such as the Industrial Revolution and the rise of computing, both of which ultimately generated more employment than they eliminated. Pessimists, however, warn that AI's ability to perform cognitive tasks fundamentally differs from earlier automation, potentially displacing workers far more broadly and rapidly.

Beyond economic concerns, AI raises profound ethical questions. The use of AI in criminal justice — particularly in algorithms that predict recidivism or assess bail — has drawn criticism for perpetuating racial and socioeconomic biases present in historical data. AI-powered surveillance systems have raised concerns about privacy and the potential for authoritarian misuse. Additionally, the environmental cost of training large AI models, which consume enormous amounts of energy, has attracted increasing attention.

Despite these concerns, there is broad consensus that AI also offers significant benefits. In healthcare, AI systems have demonstrated remarkable accuracy in detecting certain cancers and rare diseases at early stages. In climate science, AI is being used to improve the precision of weather forecasts and to model the complex dynamics of climate change. The challenge for society, therefore, is to govern AI development in ways that maximize its benefits while minimizing its risks.
