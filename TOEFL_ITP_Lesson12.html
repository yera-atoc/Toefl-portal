📄 Текст 1 · ~280 слов · Прочитай перед ответами

For centuries, astronomers relied on optical telescopes to observe the universe, limited to detecting only the narrow band of electromagnetic radiation visible to the human eye. The development of radio astronomy in the 1930s fundamentally changed this situation by opening an entirely new window on the cosmos. Radio telescopes can detect radio waves emitted by astronomical objects across vast distances, revealing phenomena that optical instruments cannot observe.

One of the most significant early discoveries of radio astronomy was the detection of quasars — extremely luminous objects found at the edges of the observable universe. Quasars emit enormous amounts of energy, far exceeding that produced by entire galaxies. Scientists now believe that quasars are powered by supermassive black holes at the centers of distant galaxies, with the black holes consuming surrounding matter and releasing tremendous energy in the process.

Radio astronomy has also contributed to our understanding of the cosmic microwave background (CMB) — the faint radiation that permeates the entire universe. This radiation is considered the afterglow of the Big Bang, the event that scientists believe created the universe approximately 13.8 billion years ago. By studying the slight temperature variations in the CMB, cosmologists have been able to piece together a detailed picture of the early universe and its subsequent evolution.

Despite the remarkable achievements of radio astronomy, the field faces significant challenges. Radio telescopes must be enormous to achieve adequate resolution, and they are vulnerable to interference from human-made radio signals. Nevertheless, advances in technology have enabled astronomers to link multiple telescopes across different continents, creating what is effectively a telescope the size of the Earth itself.

Текст 2 — Социология / Урбанизация

📄 Текст 2 · ~290 слов · Прочитай перед ответами

Urbanization — the process by which populations move from rural to urban areas — has been one of the most transformative demographic shifts of the modern era. In 1800, only three percent of the world's population lived in cities. Today, more than half of humanity resides in urban areas, and projections suggest that this proportion will rise to nearly seventy percent by 2050.

The driving forces behind urbanization are primarily economic. Cities concentrate industries, services, and employment opportunities in ways that rural areas typically cannot match. Migrants from rural regions are drawn to cities by the prospect of higher wages, better access to education, and improved healthcare facilities. This phenomenon, known as rural-to-urban migration, has been particularly pronounced in developing nations across Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

However, rapid urbanization has also generated significant social and environmental challenges. Many cities in the developing world struggle with inadequate housing, insufficient infrastructure, and overwhelmed public services. The growth of informal settlements — commonly known as slums — represents one of the most visible consequences of unmanaged urban expansion. These areas often lack basic amenities such as clean water, sanitation, and reliable electricity.

Despite these challenges, many urban planners and economists argue that well-managed cities offer the most sustainable path toward global development. Dense urban environments are inherently more energy-efficient than dispersed rural populations, as people living in close proximity require fewer resources for transportation and heating. Furthermore, cities foster innovation through the concentration of educated workers and diverse industries. The key challenge facing the twenty-first century, therefore, is not urbanization itself but ensuring that it proceeds in an equitable and sustainable manner.
